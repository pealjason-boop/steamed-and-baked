Chapter One

Sex on the Beach

Harold had been to the beach plenty of times. None of those times had involved a submarine.

Harold, approximately four hours before he found himself on a submarine.

The drink was a Sex on the Beach. Harold knew this because he had ordered it himself, carefully, syllable by syllable, from a bartender who had the look of a man who had heard everything twice and found none of it interesting. The umbrella was pink. The glass was sweating. Harold had been staring at it for eleven minutes without drinking it, which felt about right for a Tuesday.

The bar was called The Anchor. It smelled of brine, old wood, and the specific brand of despair that accumulates in places where people come to forget things and end up remembering them harder. A ceiling fan moved the air from one side of the room to the other without improving it. Harold watched it spin and thought, not for the first time that week, that his life had taken a direction he hadn't voted for.

He was thirty-one years old, five foot six in shoes, and currently between jobs in the same way that a ship is between ports when it is sitting on the ocean floor. He had come to the coast because a website had told him the saltwater would “reset his nervous system.” He had arrived to find grey skies, a closed seafood restaurant, and a beach that smelled of low tide and poor decisions.

• • •

The man who sat down next to Harold had hair the colour of gunmetal and a beard that had clearly made its own decisions years ago. His arms rested on the bar like two lengths of rigging cable. He was wearing a tan work shirt with the sleeves rolled to the elbow, revealing tattoos that seemed to tell a very long story in a language Harold didn’t speak. He smelled of diesel and something older than diesel.

“You going to drink that?” the man said, nodding at the Sex on the Beach.

Harold looked at it. “Eventually.”

“Man who drinks eventually isn’t a man with plans.”

“That’s accurate,” Harold said.

The man ordered something brown without naming it, drank half of it, and set the glass down with the care of someone setting a fuse. He looked at Harold the way people look at useful objects they haven’t decided what to do with yet.

“I’m Cutter,” he said. “I run a boat.”

“Harold. I don’t.”

The Crew of the Sea Rat

Left to right: Dutch, Harold, Cutter & Remy. Photographed approximately forty minutes before things went badly wrong.

The other two arrived shortly after, as if on cue, which Harold would later learn they were. The first was a lean, red-haired man named Remy who had the kind of face that suggested he had lost arguments with his own decisions and bore the marks of it. He wore a white vest and the expression of someone who had been awake too long on purpose. The second was large, blond, and slow in the specific way of someone who was not slow at all but had learned that the world moved faster when it thought you were stupid. He introduced himself as Dutch and was already holding a cigar he hadn’t lit, as if he was waiting for a more appropriate disaster to light it at.

“We need a fourth,” Cutter said. “Someone who doesn’t know enough to say no. You have that quality in abundance.”

Harold looked around the bar as though a better fourth might materialise from somewhere. The bartender had his back turned. A television in the corner was showing a weather forecast for somewhere else. Outside, the grey sea pushed at the grey shore with all the enthusiasm of a bureaucrat processing forms.

“What kind of boat?” Harold asked.

Cutter finished his drink. “The kind that goes under.”

• • •

The Sea Rat. Forty-three metres of bad intentions and questionable maintenance.

The submarine was called the Sea Rat, which Harold felt was doing a lot of honest work as a name. It sat in a sheltered cove about a mile down the coast, half-beached on a rock shelf, illuminated by a searchlight that had no business being operational. It was grey, dented, and bore a crest on its conning tower that looked like something a secret society would design if the secret society had very poor graphic design resources and a fondness for rats.

“Is it legal?” Harold asked.

“That’s a very broad question,” Remy said.

“It’s a yes or no question.”

Remy considered this. “It’s a spectrum question dressed up as a yes or no question. Which is the worst kind.”

Harold stood on the rocks in his swim trunks — he hadn’t had time to change, or been given time, it was hard to say — and looked at the submarine and thought about the Sex on the Beach he had left on the bar. He had paid for it. That was maybe the worst part. He had paid four euros fifty for a drink he had never drunk, and now he was standing on a wet rock at dusk being recruited by three men whose combined legal standing he estimated at approximately negative.

Cutter put a hand on his shoulder. It was a large hand, and it landed with the weight of a decision already made.

“There’s something in the water,” Cutter said quietly, staring at the submarine the way men stare at things they have staked everything on. “Something that doesn’t belong to anyone. Which means it belongs to whoever finds it first.”

“What kind of something?”

“The kind,” said Dutch, finally lighting his cigar, “that people have been looking for a very long time.”

The sea moved against the rocks. The submarine groaned like a building settling. Harold looked at the three of them — weathered, certain, slightly terrifying — and then at himself: thirty-one, between everything, holding nothing.

He got on the submarine.

He would think about why, later. At length. In the dark, in a bunk that smelled of rust and someone else’s past, listening to the ocean pressing in from all sides, he would think about why. And the closest he would ever come to an answer was this: because the drink had been called Sex on the Beach, and he had ordered it alone, and he had not drunk it, and sometimes that is enough.
